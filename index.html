Diagnostic Analytics

Why did these problems happen? Root cause analysis.

Enrollment Decline
Repeater Surge
Diagnostic — Enrollment
Root Causes of Enrollment Decline
  • Financial hardship — parents decide not to continue children's education
  • Relocation — families moving away from the service area
  • Early pregnancy — primarily affecting female students in upper grades
  • Peer pressure — influencing attendance and continuation decisions
  • Limited SHS track portfolio — no STEM, no ICT
  • Grade 10 attrition — students seek diverse strands at other schools
Impact Pattern
Grade-Level Vulnerability
  • Grade 7 — volatile; dropped to 160 then partially recovered
  • Grade 11 — lost 33.3% of students; worst decline in school
  • Grade 10 → 11 — highest attrition point
  • School relies on passive enrollment habits not active marketing
  • No community needs assessment or strand demand survey
The 5 Whys — Enrollment Decline
Root cause chain analysis
Why did the school lose 378 students over five years?
Fewer new students are entering Grade 7, and many Grade 10 completers choose to leave for SHS at other schools.
Why are Grade 10 students leaving and Grade 7 numbers dropping?
Grade 10 students seek a variety of strands not available in SRNHS, while incoming Grade 7 students face financial problems causing parents not to enroll them.
Why do Senior High students transfer to another school?
Because SRNHS has a very limited SHS portfolio — only HUMSS, ABM, HE, and Maritime — completely missing highly demanded tracks like STEM or ICT.
Why hasn't the school updated its curriculum tracks?
Because the administration has not conducted regular community surveys or market assessments to determine what tracks local industries and students actually need.
Why hasn't the school checked what the community needs?
Because the school relies on passive, traditional enrollment habits — assuming students will enroll simply because the school is nearby, rather than actively marketing and updating programs.
Diagnostic — Repeaters
Root Causes of Grade 8 & 9 Repeater Spike
  • Students neglecting studies to spend time gaming or hanging out
  • Lenient elementary grading — students pass without adequate academic habits
  • Difficulty spike in Grades 8–9 with no preparation
  • Students fall behind early in Q1, making Q4 recovery impossible
  • No Early Warning System to catch failing students in Q1
Peak Year Analysis
SY 2023–2024: The Crisis Year
  • Total repeaters reached 41 — up from 20 in baseline year
  • Repeater rate hit 3.49% — highest in 5 years
  • Grade 8: 12 repeaters (doubled from prior year)
  • Grade 9: 12 repeaters (same as Grade 8)
  • Grades 8 and 9 together = 58.5% of all repeaters that year
The 5 Whys — Repeater Surge
Root cause chain analysis for high Grade 8 & 9 repeater rates
Why did so many students fail and have to repeat Grades 8 and 9?
Because they failed core difficult subjects like Mathematics (Algebra) and Science.
Why did they fail these specific subjects?
Because they fell too far behind early in the school year, making it impossible to catch up and pass by the final grading period.
Why did they fall so far behind in their lessons?
Because they neglected their studies and chose to spend their free time gaming with friends or hanging out instead of reviewing.
Why did they think they could neglect studies and still pass?
Because they grew used to lenient grading in elementary school, where they were passed easily without having to study hard.
Why didn't the school step in before habits caused failure?
Because the school has no Early Warning System to catch struggling students during Q1. Without early detection, teachers do not realize students are failing until it is already too late to intervene.

Forecast 1 — Enrollment
Total Enrollment Below 1,000
At the current average decline of ~94 students/year, enrollment will drop to approximately 900–950 by SY 2027–2028. This risks reduced government per-student budget allocation and potentially excess teacher capacity.
Forecast 2 — SHS Attrition
Grade 11 May Drop to 110–120 Students
If JHS-to-SHS transition rates remain unchanged, Grade 11 enrollment could fall to approximately 110–120 students by SY 2027–2028. SHS sustainability becomes difficult to justify without structural strand diversification.
Forecast 3 — Repeater Rates
Rates Stabilized Above Baseline
Without intervention, the repeater rate is projected to hold at 2.10–2.25% — persistently above the 1.45% pre-pandemic baseline. This is not a recovery; it is a new, elevated normal.
Forecast 4 — Near-Completion Dropouts
Elevated Risk of Late-Stage Dropouts
Students who fail and repeat Grades 8–9 are at elevated risk of losing academic motivation. If not caught early, these students may continue struggling into Grades 10–11, increasing the likelihood of near-completion dropouts.

Prescriptive Analytics

What should be done? Evidence-based, teacher-feasible recommendations.

Enrollment Actions
Repeater Interventions
Decision Matrix
Recommendation 1
🏫 Homeroom Guidance for Career Counseling
Grade 10 homeroom advisers should use existing, mandatory DepEd Homeroom Guidance periods in January to explain how current tracks lead directly to real careers. Frame SHS as a career launchpad. Cost: Zero — uses existing scheduled periods.
Recommendation 2
🚶 Teacher-Led Feeder School Visits
JHS teachers coordinate with local elementary principals to visit Grade 6 classrooms for just 15 minutes before enrollment season. Distribute simple enrollment flyers and speak directly with upcoming students. Creates personal awareness before other schools recruit them.
Recommendation 3
📋 Early Strand Decision Survey
Grade 10 advisers print a simple one-page survey during Q2 asking: "Do you plan to stay at SRNHS for SHS? If not, why?" This gives a 2-quarter window to intervene before final decisions are made.
Long-Term Suggestion
📚 Propose STEM or ICT Track Addition
The single highest-impact strategic move is proposing a STEM or ICT track to DepEd. This directly addresses why Grade 10 students leave. Advocacy can begin with community demand data from the survey above.
Recommendation 1
🚨 Early Warning System (EWS)
Subject teachers use a shared Google Sheet or notebook to flag students who fail the first 2 quizzes or miss 3 assignments in Month 1. Flagged students receive immediate adviser attention — before they fall too far behind to recover.
Recommendation 2
👥 Peer Tutoring Club
Teachers assign high-performing Grade 10 or SHS students to tutor struggling Grade 8 and 9 students during designated remedial hours. Peer tutors often communicate more effectively with students of similar age — and it builds leadership for the tutors.
Recommendation 3
📝 PTA Academic Commitment Agreement
Once a student is flagged by the EWS, the adviser calls the parent for a structured meeting. The teacher, parent, and student all sign an Academic Commitment Agreement acknowledging the issue and committing to specific behavioral changes.
Supporting Evidence
📊 Why These Work
  • Zero-cost — no budget required
  • Teacher-controlled — no admin approval needed to start
  • Proactive — act in Q1, not Q4 when it's too late
  • Directly address root causes identified in the 5 Whys
